Finding Peace in Mountain Solitude

By Emma Richardson June 12, 2025 5 min read

The mountains have always called to me in a way that's difficult to explain to those who haven't felt it—that strange mixture of intimidation and invitation, of challenge and comfort.

Last weekend, I finally answered that call. After months of planning (or perhaps more accurately, months of procrastinating on planning), I packed my small backpack with only the essentials: a change of clothes, a water bottle, some trail mix, and a journal. The rest, I decided, I would figure out along the way.

The Journey Begins

The trail started gently enough, winding through a forest of pine trees that filtered the morning sunlight into patterns on the path. The air was crisp and carried the scent of earth and vegetation—so different from the city air I'd grown accustomed to. Within the first hour, I could feel my mind beginning to shift, the constant mental chatter giving way to something quieter, more attentive.

I passed a few fellow hikers early on, exchanging brief nods or "good mornings," but as I climbed higher, the trail grew steeper and the encounters fewer. By midday, I found myself completely alone, with only the occasional call of a bird to remind me that I shared this space with other living beings.

Mountains are not stadiums where I satisfy my ambition to achieve, they are cathedrals where I practice my religion.

— Anatoli Boukreev

The sense of solitude wasn't what I had expected. Rather than feeling isolated or anxious, there was a profound peace in knowing that in that moment, no one needed anything from me. No emails required responses, no meetings demanded preparation, no social obligations asked me to be anyone other than exactly who I was. The mountain didn't care about my job title or my accomplishments—it simply invited me to be present.

Finding Clarity in Solitude

As the trail became more challenging, requiring full attention to navigate safely, I found myself slipping into a meditative state. Each step became deliberate, each breath deeper and more satisfying than the last. My mind, usually racing from one thought to the next, began to slow down and match the rhythm of my footsteps.

By late afternoon, I reached a small plateau that offered a breathtaking view of the valley below. I sat on a sun-warmed rock, pulled out my journal, and began to write. The words came easily, without the self-consciousness that often accompanies my writing. I wrote about nothing particularly profound—just observations about the journey, the quality of the light as it slanted through the trees, the way my body felt both tired and energized.

But in those simple observations, I found insights about my life that had eluded me in more deliberate attempts at reflection. Something about the physical distance from my everyday life created a mental distance that allowed for clearer seeing.

Lessons from the Mountain

As the sun began to lower in the sky, I reluctantly packed up my journal and began the descent. The path down offered new perspectives—both literally in terms of the views and figuratively in the reflections it prompted. Here are a few of the insights that have stayed with me:

  • Patience is non-negotiable. The mountain doesn't care about your schedule. You move at the pace the terrain allows, nothing more and nothing less.
  • Preparation matters, but so does adaptability. No amount of planning can account for everything you'll encounter.
  • Discomfort is temporary and often purposeful. The burning in my legs on steep sections wasn't something to avoid but to embrace—it was the feeling of becoming stronger.
  • Beauty exists at every scale. From the sweeping vistas to the tiny wildflower growing between rocks, there are wonders to be found when you adjust your focus.

Returning Changed

I returned to my everyday life physically tired but mentally refreshed. The challenges I'd left behind suddenly seemed more manageable, not because they had changed, but because I had. The mountain had offered me both perspective and presence—two gifts I hadn't realized how desperately I needed.

In the days since my return, I've tried to maintain some of that mountain mindset. I've been taking short walks without my phone, creating small pockets of solitude in my daily routine. I've been writing more regularly, not waiting for the perfect conditions or the profound thought, but simply recording my observations and allowing insights to emerge organically.

Most importantly, I've been reminding myself that while I can't always be on the mountain, I can carry its lessons with me wherever I go. The peace I found wasn't just about the location—it was about a way of being that's available to me anytime I choose to access it.

The mountains will always call to me, and I'll answer when I can. But in the meantime, I'm learning that solitude and mindfulness can be found even in the midst of a busy life—perhaps especially there, where they're needed most.
